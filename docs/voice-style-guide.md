# Wisco Radio Labs — Voice & Style Guide

The reference the copywriter and every blog post build from, so the site sounds like
**Travis (K9MTE)** — not like an agency. Informed by Travis's own writing. This describes
the **public, non-fiction register**: the brand face for a largely licensed, often older,
family-grade ham/maker audience. (No fiction text is reproduced here — this is the
abstracted fingerprint only.)

## The voice in one line
A Driftless maker telling you the truth, plainly, with dry humor and exact detail — and
never pretending.

## Two registers (and where each belongs)
- **Plain & direct — PRIMARY, use everywhere.** First-person, conversational, specific,
  dry-funny, honest, understated. The workbench-with-the-door-open voice. Home, About,
  Products, blog body, how-to.
- **Lyrical & crafted — SPARING.** Vivid, image-led, slower, resonant. Small doses only — a
  blog post's opening line, a short reflective aside. Never for product or how-to copy.

## DO — the fingerprint
- **Write in first person, to one reader.** "Here's what I built and why," never "Wisco
  Radio Labs is pleased to announce."
- **Be specific to the point of proper nouns.** Real names, real numbers, real gear.
  Specificity is the whole trick — it's what makes it true. (`snap install wr-cw-trainer`,
  "Koch method," "On Linux now" — keep doing exactly this.)
- **Vary the rhythm; end with a punch.** A long momentum sentence, then a short blunt one. A
  three-word sentence lands harder than a paragraph.
- **Dry, self-deprecating humor. Understate.** Let the reader smile; don't perform for them.
- **Be honest about what's hard or unfinished** — matter-of-factly, no melodrama, no spin.
  "I built it. Then I built it again, better."
- **Revere the Driftless and the craft** — place and process, accurately. "Build it well.
  Make it repairable. Show the work." is the voice *and* the doctrine.
- **Earn sentiment, then ground it.** A genuine feeling is fine; undercut it a touch so it
  never goes saccharine.

## DON'T — translating his fiction to the brand
Travis's fiction is raw, profane, and goes to dark places. The public brand keeps the
*spirit* of that honesty and humor and drops:
- **Profanity: follow the Language line below** (clean brand pages; a little earned salt OK
  in the blog). The realness comes from specificity and candor, not swearing.
- **No heavy/dark or personal subject matter.** His hardship is his to tell, if ever; the
  site is about the work.
- **No rambling runway.** His fiction can wander beautifully; web copy can't. Keep the voice,
  cut the length — get to it.
- **No agency-speak, ever.** No "revolutionary / seamless / cutting-edge / we're excited to,"
  no press-release cadence. It's the opposite of him, and this audience punishes it.
- **No hype or overpromising.** (The `brand-truth-reviewer` gate enforces this regardless —
  shipped facts only.)

## Language line (Travis's decision, 2026-06-29)
- **Brand pages — Home, Products, About, Contact: ZERO profanity.** These are the public
  product face for a family-grade, often older, licensed ham audience. Keep the edge through
  candor, specificity, and dry humor — never through swearing.
- **Blog: a little earned salt is OK.** The blog is Travis's personal space. The occasional
  mild word ("hell," "damn," "ass," "crap," "pissed") is fine *when it's earned and
  in-character* — seasoning, not the meal. Hard limits even on the blog: **no f-bombs, no
  slurs, ever; never gratuitous; never in a post title, heading, or opening line** (those
  surface in nav, search, RSS, and social cards). When in doubt, leave it out.
- The `brand-truth-reviewer` gate enforces this line on every piece, by surface.

## Optional motifs (borrowed from his fiction, used lightly)
- **An epigraph opening line** for a blog post — one short, resonant sentence before the
  piece begins. He does this in his fantasy work; sparingly, it can set a post's tone.
- **The em-dash aside and the parenthetical** — natural to him; fine in moderation.

## Sounds like Travis / doesn't (original examples, not his fiction)
- ✗ "Wisco Radio Labs is proud to deliver a revolutionary, seamless Morse-learning experience."
- ✓ "I wanted to learn CW the right way and couldn't find a tool that did it. So I built one.
  It's on Linux now."

## Gut check before anything ships
Read it back as if Travis said it out loud at the bench. If it sounds like a brochure, a
LinkedIn post, or someone trying to sound smart — it's wrong. If it sounds like a
straight-talking maker who'd rather show you than sell you — it's right.
