# Wisco Radio Labs — Website Marketing Plan

Strategy and intent for the Wisco Radio Labs (K9MTE) website — the owned brand
surface. This is the **plan the copywriter and web-designer build against**, not the
copy and not the images. It marks the brand, the person, and the mission — *not* an
unshipped product.

> **Scope + policy (read first).** This plans the **website and the brand**, an owned
> surface. It is **not an external ad campaign.** No active outbound publishing to ham
> forums / Reddit / YouTube / Facebook is planned here — that push waits for Travis's
> explicit launch go. Where this doc touches channels, it describes **readiness and how
> the site connects out**, never a campaign activation. The brand-truth hard lines hold
> absolutely: only real, shipped facts (the CW Trainer is genuinely live on the Snap
> Store at v2.3.0 — describe only what ships; roadmap is labeled as roadmap). Every
> piece passes the `brand-truth-reviewer` gate before it reaches Travis, who owns
> publishing.

> **Relationship to the build.** This plan **builds on** the existing design system
> (`docs/design-system.md`) and layouts (`docs/layouts.md`) — it does not fight them.
> The design language ("clean, legible, honest, a little understated — a maker's bench,
> not a billboard") *is already the marketing voice in visual form.* That alignment is
> the asset; keep brand and build reading as one thing.

## 1. Brand positioning

**Who.** Wisco Radio Labs is one licensed amateur operator — **Travis Engh, K9MTE** —
building honest, open-source tools for the ham-radio community, in the open, from the
Driftless region of Wisconsin. It is not a company with a marketing department. It is a
workbench with the door open. (Brand-history note: "Labs" is load-bearing — the brand
was disambiguated from an existing Wisconsin sports-radio company; keep "Labs" present
wherever the name appears.)

**The mission.** Advance the radio art and contribute to the technical knowledge of the
hobby by building things hams can actually use — and sharing them freely. This is the
hobby's own stated purpose, not marketing language: it aligns honestly with **FCC Part
97.1(b)** (*"continuation and extension of the amateur's proven ability to contribute to
the advancement of the radio art"*) and **97.1(c)** (*"advancing skills in both the
communication and technical phases of the art"*) — a CW trainer that builds operator
skill is squarely on-mission for 97.1(c).
> Treat the 97.1 tie as **genuine alignment, never a regulatory claim or endorsement.**
> Phrase it as "in the spirit of," not "certified/approved." This is a Travis-confirm
> item (see §8) and a hard gate item.

**The true story (because it's true, it's the brand).** A working ham, with military-
maintenance discipline and a maker's instinct, builds the tools he wished existed,
tests them on his own key and bench, ships them free and open-source, and writes up
the work — wins and dead-ends both. The CW Trainer is the first one out the door: he
built the Morse trainer he wanted, and it's live.

**Values (the four that show up in everything):**
- **Honest** — only real, shipped claims; failures shown alongside wins. (This is also
  the build doctrine; the site should embody it.)
- **Open** — free, GPL-3.0, fully offline, built in public.
- **Specific** — made by an operator who uses the thing, for a real use on the air —
  not generic.
- **Of a place** — *Made in the Driftless.* A real region, a real bench, a real person.

**Positioning line (internal — not copy):** *Tools made by a ham who actually uses
them, shared in the open, from the Driftless.*

## 2. Audience

Two real audiences, served by one site without diluting either.

**A. Practicing hams & makers — the core (value-first).**
- *Who:* licensed operators, CW ops, kit builders, POTA/SOTA/field operators, tinkerers.
  They built much of open-source radio and they **revere genuine value and revile a
  sales pitch.**
- *What they want from the site:* real builds and honest write-ups; a working, free tool
  that helps them on the air; transparency about what's done and what isn't; a way to
  follow the work (RSS/YouTube) and to reach a real person. Mostly reading **on a phone**,
  at the bench or in the shack at night (the dark-first design is for them).
- *How the site serves them:* the **Blog/vlog** is the center of gravity; the **CW
  Trainer** product page is the proof; the **About** page is the trust anchor (a real
  operator, a real callsign).

**B. Curious newcomers & aspiring CW learners — the funnel.**
- *Who:* people learning Morse, studying for a license, or radio-curious makers who
  landed on a build post. Lower jargon tolerance, higher need for "what is this / where
  do I start."
- *What they want:* an approachable on-ramp, jargon glossed on first use (CW, Koch, QSO,
  73), and a clear reason the hobby and the tool are worth their time.
- *How the site serves them:* the **CW-craft content pillar** (§5) is their entry, and
  the **CW Trainer** is their first tool. They are a *secondary, on-site* funnel — the
  site welcomes them; we do **not** plan a separate short-form outbound push here.

> One site, two reading levels: write for the ham, gloss for the newcomer. Never dumb it
> down for the core; never lock out the beginner. The design system already bakes this
> in ("gloss ham jargon on first use").

## 3. Voice & tone guide (for the site)

**The one line:** write like a competent ham talking to another ham at the bench —
plain, specific, a little understated, honest about what's unfinished. The brand is a
**person**; default to **first person ("I built…")**, not corporate third person
("Wisco Radio Labs is proud to announce…").

**Do**
- Lead with the real thing: what it is, what it does, what it cost you to make.
- Use correct ham/CW terms (prosigns, *73*, callsign etiquette, band/mode facts) — and
  gloss them on first use for newcomers.
- Show the work, including dead-ends and "here's what didn't work."
- Be specific: "Koch-method character lessons and a QSO simulator, fully offline" beats
  "a powerful learning platform."
- Sign off like an operator. *73* is on-brand and true.

**Don't**
- No hype or superlatives — *revolutionary, game-changing, ultimate, seamless, next-gen.*
- No claiming anything not shipped (no roadmap-as-fact, no "coming soon" dressed as
  "available").
- No press-release voice, no fake scarcity, no growth-hacking CTAs ("Don't miss out!").
- No filler that could belong to any project (the "specific over generic" pillar).
- Don't over-explain to the point of condescension — glosses are brief asides, not
  lectures.

**Sample phrasings (direction, not final copy):**
- Hero: *"Ham radio, built in the open."* (endorse the existing layout line — it is the
  brand in five words.)
- CW Trainer: *"Learn Morse the way hams actually use it — free, open source, and fully
  offline."*
- About: *"I'm Travis, K9MTE. I build radio tools I wish existed, then give them away."*
- Avoid: *"Wisco Radio Labs delivers cutting-edge solutions for the modern amateur."*

**Tone shift by surface:**
| Surface | Voice |
|---|---|
| **Home** | Welcoming, brand-level, confident-but-quiet. One clear "what this is," then point to the blog and the tool. The badge does the rest. |
| **Blog/vlog** | Most personal and in-the-weeds — the workbench voice. Narrative, first person, technical, honest about failures. This is where the brand lives. |
| **Products / CW Trainer** | Precise and factual. Exactly what it does, exactly where to get it, honest line between shipped and roadmap. Calm confidence, zero hype. |
| **About** | Warmest and most personal — the *why*. The story of a ham who builds and gives away. Military-maintenance discipline + maker's instinct + place. |
| **Contact** | Direct and human. "Questions, ideas, a bug? Here's how to reach a real person." Sign off *73.* |

## 5. Website content & social strategy

All on-site / owned-surface. **Outbound publishing stays dark** — this section describes
content the site holds and how it *connects out* when Travis gives the go, not a posting
schedule for forums/Reddit/YouTube.

### 5.1 Blog/vlog content pillars (four — keep it tight)
1. **Builds** — what Travis is making: the CW Trainer dev notes, and future projects.
   The flagship pillar; this is the brand's reason to exist.
2. **CW & operating craft** — Morse, the Koch method, sending/copying practice, on-air
   operating. *This is also the newcomer funnel and the SEO engine* (people search
   "learn Morse code," "Koch method practice," "CW QSO").
3. **Field & the Driftless** — POTA/SOTA, portable ops, antennas in trees, the place
   itself. Ties "Made in the Driftless" to real ground.
4. **Bench notes** — tools, fixes, process, the maintenance-discipline angle. Short,
   useful, frequent-friendly.

### 5.2 Cadence (a light recommendation, not a mandate)
Publish **when there's something real**, target a sustainable rhythm (≈2 posts/month is
plenty to start). **Quality and honesty over volume** — a content-mill cadence would
violate the honesty pillar and the audience would smell it. One genuine build write-up
beats four manufactured posts. The vlog rides on the same cadence (a post can embed a
YouTube video; not every post needs one).

### 5.3 The "social aspect" (on-site patterns, all owned)
- **RSS** — already ON. Make it *discoverable*: an autodiscovery `<link>` in `<head>`,
  plus a visible RSS link in header/footer (the design system already places one in the
  footer). RSS is how this audience actually follows a maker blog.
- **Follow/subscribe surfaces** — RSS now; **YouTube subscribe** once the channel URL
  exists (pending from Travis); an optional **email newsletter** (recommended *yes, but
  later* — see §8; low-frequency, opt-in, privacy-friendly, needs a service = Travis's
  vendor/budget call).
- **Share affordances** — keep minimal and privacy-clean: a native "share"/copy-link per
  post, **no third-party share/tracking widgets** (matches the privacy-friendly-analytics
  intent). The *real* share lever is the **OG/Twitter card** (§6) — when a ham drops your
  link anywhere, it must unfurl looking sharp and on-brand. That's the highest-leverage
  "social" investment and it's [BUILDABLE].
- **Outbound linking (readiness, not a campaign)** — the site links *out* to YouTube,
  QRZ (K9MTE), and socials from the footer/contact. The **site is the hub; channels
  orbit it.** Wiring those links is readiness; *pushing* content to those channels waits
  for Travis's go.

### 5.4 Discoverability / SEO (strategic level)
- **Already strong:** semantic HTML, sitemap + RSS generated, per-page titles/descriptions.
- **Add (all [BUILDABLE], cheap, high-value):**
  - **OG/Twitter card tags per page** + a default card image (§6) — the shareable face.
  - **JSON-LD structured data:** `Person` (Travis Engh / K9MTE), `BlogPosting` per
    article, `SoftwareApplication` for the CW Trainer. Helps search understand the brand,
    the author, and the product.
  - The **CW-craft pillar is the organic-search engine** — newcomers searching to learn
    Morse are exactly the funnel, and that content earns its ranking honestly.
- **Stays dark until go-live:** `noindex` is correctly ON now. SEO is *readiness work* —
  built and ready, switched live with the domain. No outbound link-building planned here.

## 6. Imagery & art-direction plan (the centerpiece)

**Hard constraint, honored.** There is **no image-generation tool** in this environment.
Every image below is tagged by how it actually gets made:
- **[BUILDABLE]** — the `web-designer` can produce it for real in SVG/CSS or as a
  templated card, on-palette and repairable.
- **[PHOTO]** — Travis supplies a real photograph (shot list given). **This is the gold
  for a ham/maker audience** — authentic bench/shack/field photos out-trust any graphic.
- **[GENERATED]** — AI-generated/commissioned. **Recommended at zero for v1** — AI-slop
  reads as inauthentic to this community and would undercut the honesty value. One
  optional candidate is flagged, with its risk.
- **[REUSE]** — an asset that already exists (logo, CW Trainer banner/screenshots); no
  new production.

**Art-direction north star.** Everything anchors to the **logo palette** (charcoal field
`#0F1115`, amber `#E8821E`, gray tower/structure) and the design language: *restraint,
borders-and-surface over heavy shadow, the signal-wave motif as the one accent, "a
maker's bench not a billboard."* The recurring brand device is the **eyebrow** (uppercase,
wide-tracked amber kicker) and the **tower/signal-wave** mark. When in doubt, remove.
